# The Filter Nobody's Watching: A Partnership Proposal

### Why the best HR teams in the market are about to have a very good problem to solve

---

You've likely seen the story by now: a hiring manager tests his own resume against his company's applicant tracking system — and it gets rejected. The entire HR team is let go. The headline reads like an isolated scandal. It isn't.

It's a symptom of something almost every company with a modern ATS is exposed to right now, quietly, without knowing it.

**Somewhere in your hiring funnel, a piece of software is making rejection decisions on your behalf — and almost no one can tell you, with certainty, why it says yes to one candidate and no to another.**

That's not a criticism of any HR team. It's the honest state of an industry that adopted automation faster than it built the discipline to govern it. The tools scaled. The oversight didn't.

We think that gap is exactly where the next competitive advantage in talent acquisition is going to be built. And we'd like to build it with you.

---

## The Problem, Plainly

Applicant tracking systems were sold as time-savers. In practice, they've become gatekeepers with no one checking their judgment.

- Filtering rules get configured once, at setup, and rarely revisited.
- No one is systematically testing whether the system still filters *correctly* as job descriptions, roles, and hiring criteria evolve.
- When the system rejects a qualified candidate, nobody finds out — because rejected candidates don't complain to HR about a decision they don't know was automated.
- The people whose reputations are on the line — recruiters, hiring managers, HR leadership — are the last to know when something's gone wrong, and usually only find out by accident, the way this headline's HR team did.

The cost isn't hypothetical. It's qualified people never seen, open roles staying open longer than they should, and — increasingly — legal and reputational exposure when filtering criteria end up correlating with age, employment gaps, or other protected characteristics nobody intended to screen for.

This is not a people problem. It's a **governance problem.** And governance problems are solvable — but only by people who understand both the human stakes of hiring and the discipline needed to keep a decision-making system honest.

That's the partnership we're proposing.

---

## What We're Proposing to Build, Together

We want to build a system that does for hiring decisions what modern financial and compliance tools have done for fraud detection and risk scoring: make an automated decision-maker **accountable, visible, and provably correct** — without slowing hiring down or making it any less automated.

In practice, that means:

**Visibility.** Every automated rejection comes with a clear, plain-language reason attached to it — not a black box. Your team can see, at a glance, why the system said no.

**Testing before trust.** Before any filtering rule goes live, it gets tested against a set of real, representative resumes — including the kind of edge cases that get quietly filtered out today: career changers, non-linear paths, employment gaps, non-traditional backgrounds. If it would have rejected your own best hire, it doesn't ship.

**Early warning, not after-the-fact discovery.** A live view of how the system is behaving — rejection rates by role, sudden shifts after a change, patterns worth a second look — so your team catches problems in days, not by accident, months later.

**A second chance, built in.** Rejected candidates aren't gone forever. They sit in a recoverable pool, so if something was wrong with the filter, nothing was permanently lost.

**A clear owner.** Someone on your team — not a vendor, not a black box — is accountable for the *quality* of what the system decides, with the tools to actually do that job well.

None of this requires ripping out your existing ATS or retraining your team from scratch. It's a layer of discipline and visibility built around the systems you already trust, closing the exact gap this week's headline exposed.
